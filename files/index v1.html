2. Flexible parametric models

Traditional survival regression models use a non-parametric approach (Cox regression), which assume non-proportional hazards across follow-up time and constant hazard ratios (comparing two exposure levels) across the duration of follow-up. A strength of this approach is that the baseline hazard (or level of risk when all covariates are zero) does not need to be estimated. However, the non-proportionality assumption may not hold in practice. Two methods can be used to account for the baseline hazard function, including parametric models (e.g., weibull, exponential) and flexible parametric survival models. Flexible parametric survival models are similar to parametric models, but allow for the baseline hazard function to be estimated flexibly using cubic splines (and thus one does not need to specify a specific distribution). By extension, the baseline hazard can be interacted with follow-up time to create smoothed plots of time-varying hazard differences on both the absolute and relative scale. This is akin to running piecewise models to estimate time-varying hazards, but with the added ability to generate continuous estimates over time without disjointed breaks placed arbitrarily. Flexible parametric models can address may problems with non-parametric and parametric models, and should be used more often in practice. The following paper applies these concepts to examine the time-varying relationship between older age and disability claim duration outcomes among injured workers, using a novel administrative claims database stitched from six Canadian provinces with data on over 750,000 injured workers.

3. Margins and predicted probabilities

When visualizing an interaction created using a non-linear model such as logistic regression, sometimes it is better to estimate the interaction effect on the predicted probability scale rather than the multiplicative scale (i.e., odds ratios or relative risks). Both scales provide complementary interpretations of any heterogeneity in estimates across the stratification variables (VanderWeele and Knol, 2014). Specifically, on the OR scale, we can examine whether the underlying relationships between treatment and outcomes are similar in magnitude and direction across levels of a third variable, which speaks to etiologic mechanisms. On the probability scale, we can examine the potential public health impact in relation to absolute levels of the outcome or health condition, given that it focuses on whether the anticipated effect of changes in a risk factor might be greater across certain subgroups. The probability scale may be relevant for the development of targeted interventions that aim to maximize the potential impact in terms of targeting specific populations with limited resources (VanderWeele and Knol, 2014), as it incorporates information on the baseline prevalence of health outcomes while identifying groups with the greatest prevalence of each condition. The following paper applies these concepts to better understand the relationship between psychosocial work conditions and mental health outcomes.

4. Causal mediation

Epidemiologic research often focuses on understanding the relationship between an exposure variable and outcome variable. However, this relationship can be influenced by three types of variables: confounders, mediators, and moderators. Confounders could bias the magnitude and direction of association of the exposure-outcome relationship. Mediators can act within the pathway between exposure and outcome. The distinction between confounders and mediators is theoretical, as they will have a statistically similar effect on the exposure-outcome relationship. Finally, the exposure-outcome relationship may differ across levels of a moderator variable, resulting in interaction or effect modification. The paper below uses structural equation modelling to examine the mediating role of functional and organizational factors in explaining the relationship between older age and RTW outcomes among injured workers, using linked survey and administrative claims data.

5. Estimating causal treatment effects in observational studies using R

The relationship between a treatment and outcome variable can be confounded by a third variable that is a common cause of both treatment and outcome. This leads to a spurious association and/or biased measure of treatment effect. To control for confounding, traditional methods include stratifying the sample by the confounder or adjusting for it in a regression model. Contemporary methods include the use of propensity scores, which are defined as an individual's probability of treatment selection, conditional on the set of baseline covariates or confounders. The propensity score can be readily calculated using a binary outcome model such as logistic regression (or multinomial regression in the case of multiple treatments), with the treatment variable entered into the model as the response variable. The propensity scores can then be used to adjust for confounding in several ways, including direct inclusion as a model covariate; stratification by the propensity score; matching using the propensity score; or creation of IPTW weights (inverse of the probability of treatment selection, i.e., inverse of the propensity scores) and subsequent use in regression models or tabulations (i.e., by making use of survey syntax). The use of propensity score-based measures neccessitates the framing of research questions in terms of causal inference language or potential outcomes, which entail the assumptions of positivity, exchangeability, consistency and no interference. The use of propensity scores is also closely related to causal mediation analysis, which implicitly invokes the assumptions of causal inference in order to identify causal treatment effects and control against confounding of various pathways.

8. SAS syntax to extract numbers from a string of text using regex

A common problem when analyzing free-text data is the need to extract numbers embedded within a string of text. For example, work hours could be reported by the respondent within a free-text box, with responses that are easy to extract (e.g. 37.5 or 40), yet other responses that are messy (e.g., "80 on call", "only a few hours, 8 per week") or contain multiple values (e.g., "80 hours over 2 weeks", "30 to 35 hours"). The key is to use a regex search pattern written as "/\.?\d+(\.\d+)*/" which searches for an optional decimal at the beginning (e.g., for hours written as ".5"), followed by one or more digits, followed by an optional decimal place with more digits. The attached SAS syntax will extract all numbers from the example "80 hours over 2 weeks", storing them in separate columns; however, the data user will still have to manually determine which column contains the hours and which column contains the week number (but at least it will be extracted in a column for manual review). Also attached is a spreadsheet to test the code against some scenarios. Extracted variables will be stored in extractedhours1-extractedhours5. It will also export a file containing the coded data and uncoded data for manual cleaning.
