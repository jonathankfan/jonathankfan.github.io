3. Causal inference and estimating treatment effects in observational studies using R
Author.
The relationship between a treatment and outcome variable can be confounded by a third variable that is a common cause of both treatment and outcome. This leads to a spurious association and/or biased measure of treatment effect. To control for confounding, traditional methods include stratifying the sample by the confounder or adjusting for it in a regression model. Contemporary methods include the use of propensity scores, which are defined as an individual's probability of treatment selection, conditional on the set of baseline covariates or confounders. The propensity score can be readily calculated using a binary outcome model such as logistic regression (or multinomial regression in the case of multiple treatments), with the treatment variable entered into the model as the response variable. The propensity scores can then be used to adjust for confounding in several ways, including direct inclusion as a model covariate; stratification by the propensity score; matching using the propensity score; or creation of inverse probability of treatment weights (IPTW) (inverse of the probability of treatment selection, i.e., inverse of the propensity scores) and subsequent use in regression models or tabulations (i.e., by making use of survey syntax). The use of propensity score-based measures neccessitates the framing of research questions in terms of causal inference language or potential outcomes, which entail the assumptions of positivity, exchangeability, consistency and no interference. The use of propensity scores is also closely related to causal mediation analysis, which implicitly invokes the assumptions of causal inference in order to identify causal treatment effects and control against confounding of various pathways.

These concepts were applied in a recent paper conducted by our research group that used propensity scores and IPTW to examine differences in the RTW process following workplace injury.

4. Causal mediation
Author.
Epidemiologic research frequently focuses on understanding the relationship between an exposure variable and outcome variable. However, this relationship can be influenced by three types of variables: confounders, mediators, and moderators. Confounders could bias the magnitude and direction of association of the exposure-outcome relationship. Mediators can act within the pathway between exposure and outcome. The distinction between confounders and mediators is largely theoretical, as they will have a statistically similar effect on the exposure-outcome relationship. Finally, the exposure-outcome relationship may differ across levels of a moderator variable, resulting in interaction or effect modification.

When examining causal mechanisms or target points for interventions, the focus shifts towards mediating variables between an exposure and outcome variable. By examining the mediating pathway, the overall relationship between an exposure and outcome can be separated into the direct effect of the exposure variable (acting directly on the outcome variable) and the indirect effect that operates via the mediating variable. We can then calculate the proportion of the overall relationship that is due to the indirect pathway (via the mediating variable) in order to assess whether targeted interventions might reduce the incidence or severity of the outcome. However, mediation analysis requires careful control against confounding of each of the various pathways in order to identify causal treatment effects, as well as various causal-inference assumptions (i.e., positivity, exchangeability, consistency and no interference). Moreover, the presence of multiple indirect pathways requires additional methodological considerations in order to disentangle the various pathways in the presence of exposure-mediator interaction effects (which poses a challenge for potential-outcome mediation approaches, but not structural equation approaches).

These concepts are applied in my recently published paper that used structural equation modelling to examine the mediating role of functional and organizational factors in explaining the relationship between older age and RTW outcomes among injured workers, using linked survey and administrative claims data.

5. Flexible parametric models
Author.
Traditional survival regression models use a non-parametric approach (Cox regression), which assume constant hazard ratios (when comparing two exposure levels) across the duration of follow-up. This is known as the proportional hazards assumption. A strength of this approach is that the baseline hazard (or level of risk when all covariates are zero) does not need to be estimated, as it is treated as a "nuisance" parameter in the models. However, the proportionality assumption may not hold in practice (rendering the single hazard ratio provided by the models as a less-accurate characterization of the nature of the relationship across follow-up time). Moreover, absolute measures of effect (e.g., risk or rate differences) rather than relative measures of effect (e.g., hazard ratios) might be desired. However, this requires calculation of the baseline hazard function to estimate the "reference" category for absolute measures of effect.

Two methods can be used to account for the baseline hazard function, including parametric models (e.g., weibull, exponential) and flexible parametric survival models. Flexible parametric survival models are similar to parametric models, but allow for the baseline hazard function to be estimated flexibly using cubic splines (and thus one does not need to specify a specific distribution as in the parametric models). By extension, the baseline hazard can be interacted with follow-up time to create smoothed plots of time-varying hazard differences on both the absolute and relative scale. This is akin to running piecewise models to estimate time-varying hazards, but with the added ability to generate continuous estimates over time without disjointed breaks placed arbitrarily. Flexible parametric models can address many problems with non-parametric and parametric models, and could be used more often in practice.

These concepts are applied in my recently published paper that examined the time-varying relationship between older age and disability claim duration outcomes among injured workers, using a novel administrative claims database stitched from six Canadian provinces with data on over 750,000 injured workers.

6. Margins and predicted probabilities
Author.
When visualizing an interaction created using a non-linear model such as logistic regression, sometimes it is better to estimate the interaction effect on the predicted probability scale rather than the multiplicative scale (i.e., odds ratios or relative risks). Both scales provide complementary interpretations of any heterogeneity in estimates across the stratification variables (VanderWeele and Knol, 2014). Specifically, on the OR scale, we can examine whether the underlying relationships between treatment and outcomes are similar in magnitude and direction across levels of a third variable, which speaks to etiologic mechanisms. On the probability scale, we can examine the potential public health impact in relation to absolute levels of the outcome or health condition, given that it focuses on whether the anticipated effect of changes in a risk factor might be greater across certain subgroups. The probability scale may be relevant for the development of targeted interventions that aim to maximize the potential impact in terms of targeting specific populations with limited resources (VanderWeele and Knol, 2014), as it incorporates information on the baseline prevalence of health outcomes while identifying groups with the greatest prevalence of each condition.

These concepts are applied in my recently published paper that examined the relationship between psychosocial work conditions and mental health outcomes using a novel application of multivariate modelling to test for heterogeneity in outcome-specific estimates on the additive and multiplicative scales.

9. SAS syntax to extract numbers from a string of text using regex
Author.
A common problem when analyzing free-text data is the need to extract numbers embedded within a string of text. For example, work hours could be reported by the respondent within a free-text box, with responses that are easy to extract (e.g. 37.5 or 40), yet other responses that are messy (e.g., "80 on call", "only a few hours, 8 per week") or contain multiple values (e.g., "80 hours over 2 weeks", "30 to 35 hours"). The key is to use a regex search pattern written as "/\.?\d+(\.\d+)*/" which searches for an optional decimal at the beginning (e.g., for hours written as ".5"), followed by one or more digits, followed by an optional decimal place with more digits. The attached SAS syntax will extract all numbers from the example "80 hours over 2 weeks", storing them in separate columns; however, the data user will still have to manually determine which column contains the hours and which column contains the week number (but at least it will be extracted in a column for manual review). Also attached is a spreadsheet to test the code against some scenarios. Extracted variables will be stored in extractedhours1-extractedhours5. It will also export a file containing the coded data and uncoded data for manual cleaning.
